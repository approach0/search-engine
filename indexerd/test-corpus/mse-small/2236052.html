Partial sums and Lesbegue integral

Let [imath]D = [-1/2,1/2]^n[/imath] be the unit cube. Let [imath]f:\mathbb{R}^n[/imath] be a function such that the Riemman integral
[imath]\int_{\rho D} f(x)\, \text{d}x[/imath]
exists for all [imath]\rho > 0.[/imath]
Under what conditions can we say that
(i)
[imath]\lim_{\rho \to \infty} \int_{\rho D} f(x) \,\text{d}x = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x) \,\text{d}x,[/imath]
passing to the Lesbegue integral on the right-hand side and
(ii) [imath]\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x)\, \text{d}x = \lim_{k\to\infty} \sum_{x \in \mathbb{Z}^n} f(x/k)k^{-n} \ ?[/imath]
For instance, for the Riemman integral it is not hard to see that:
[imath] \int_{\rho D} f(x) \,\text{d}x = \lim_{k\to\infty} \sum_{x \in \mathbb{Z}^n} f(x/k)k^{-n}\mathbb{1}_{\rho(D)}(x) [/imath]


Part (i)
If [imath]f[/imath] is Lebesgue integrable over [imath]\mathbb{R}^n[/imath] (which requires absolute integrability) then
[imath]-\infty < \int_{\mathbb{R}^n}f = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n}f^+ - \int_{\mathbb{R}^n}f^- < +\infty.[/imath]
where the positive and negative parts are [imath]f^+(x) = \max(f(x),0)[/imath] and [imath]f^-(x) = -\min(f(x), 0),[/imath] and individually
[imath]0 \leqslant \int_{\mathbb{R}^n}f^+ < +\infty, \\ 0 \leqslant \int_{\mathbb{R}^n}f^- < +\infty.[/imath]
The Riemann and Lebesgue integrals coincide on bounded rectangles. Hence, by the monotone convergence theorem
[imath]\lim_{\rho \to \infty} \int_{\rho D} f^+(x) \, dx = \lim_{\rho \to \infty} \int_{\rho D} f^+ = \lim_{\rho \to \infty} \int_{\mathbb{R^n}} f^+ \chi_{\rho D} = \int_{\mathbb{R^n}} f^+ .[/imath]
A similar result holds for [imath]f^-[/imath] and, consequently
[imath]\lim_{\rho \to \infty} \int_{\rho D} f(x) \, dx = \int_{\mathbb{R^n}} f. [/imath]
If [imath]f[/imath] is not Lebesgue integrable over [imath]\mathbb{R}^n[/imath], but Riemann integrable over bounded rectangles, then the limit on the left might converge to an improper Riemann integral, despite the non-existence of a finite Lebesgue integral. For [imath]n=1[/imath], the typical example is [imath]f(x) = \sin x / x[/imath].
Part (ii)
It seems you are asking if the limit of Riemann sums over an expanding region can be evaluated as a double limit with the order switched and converge to the integral over the infinite domain.
For [imath]n = 1[/imath] and integration over [imath][0,\infty)[/imath] this would translate to
[imath]\lim_{k \to \infty} \lim_{m \to \infty} k^{-1} \sum_{j=1}^{mk} f(j/k) = \lim_{m \to \infty} \lim_{k \to \infty} k^{-1} \sum_{j=1}^{mk} f(j/k) = \lim_{m \to \infty} \int_0^m f(x) \, dx\\ = \int_0^\infty f(x) \, dx[/imath]
I surmise this can be justified by some form of monotone or dominated convergence.
