First, the notations [imath]f_x', f_y', f_x''[/imath], and [imath]f_y''[/imath] aren't correct. You're mixing derivative notations. The prime notation ([imath]'[/imath], [imath]''[/imath], etc.) is used for functions of one variable. The subscript notation is (typically) used for functions of multiple variables. So just say [imath]f_x[/imath] and [imath]f_y[/imath] for first partials with respect to [imath]x[/imath] and [imath]y[/imath], respectively. The second partial with respect to [imath]x[/imath] is [imath]f_{xx}[/imath], with a similar meaning for [imath]f_{yy}[/imath].
You correctly found that [imath]x^2 = y^2[/imath] must be true in order to have critical points. Now you want to substitute that back into each of your original equations. Note that it may be more useful to use the equivalent forms [imath]x = \pm y[/imath] or [imath]y = \pm x[/imath] instead of [imath]x^2 = y^2[/imath].
Let's substitute [imath]x = y[/imath] into both equations. Then we get:
\begin{align*}
f_x &= -y^2 + 2y^2 - 1 = 0\\
f_y &= y^2 -2y^2 + 1 = 0
\end{align*}
From both we get [imath]y^2 = 1[/imath], and so [imath]y = \pm 1[/imath]. Recall that this is the case of [imath]x^2=y^2[/imath] where [imath]x=y[/imath]. This means [imath]x = 1[/imath] when [imath]y=1[/imath] and [imath]x=-1[/imath] when [imath]y=-1[/imath]. So from this case we get the two points [imath](1,1)[/imath] and [imath](-1,-1)[/imath].
Now let's substitute [imath]x=-y[/imath] into both equations. Then we get
\begin{align*}
f_x &= -y^2 - 2y^2 - 1 = 0\\
f_y &= y^2 +2y^2 + 1 = 0
\end{align*}
From both we get [imath]3y^2 = -1[/imath], which means there are no solutions from this case.
Note also that [imath](0,0)[/imath] is not a solution because [imath]f_x(0,0) = -1 \ne 0[/imath] and [imath]f_y(0,0) = 1 \ne 0[/imath].
Anyway, now that you have your only two critical points [imath](1,1)[/imath] and [imath](-1,-1)[/imath], you want to do the second derivative test for functions of two variables.
