There is actually. Because puntual convergence just gives u the behaviour of the functions at one point but not on the whole. For example take [imath]f_n:[0,1]\rightarrow [0,1] [/imath] where [imath]f_n(x)=x^n[/imath] then work out the puntual limit of [imath]f_n[/imath] and u'll see the limit function suddenly becomes discontinuous. I guess you were to understand the difference in the requirements of the two modes of convergence of a sequence of functions. Let [imath]X \neq \varnothing[/imath]; let [imath](M,d)[/imath] be a metric space; let [imath]f_{n}: X \to M[/imath] for all integers [imath]n \geq 1[/imath]; let [imath]f: X \to M[/imath]. Then the sequence [imath](f_{n})[/imath] is said to converge pointwisely to [imath]f[/imath] iff for every [imath]x \in X[/imath] and every [imath]\varepsilon > 0[/imath] there is some integer [imath]N \geq 1[/imath] such that [imath]n \geq N[/imath] implies [imath]d(f_{n}(x),f(x)) < \varepsilon[/imath]. Clearly, pointwise convergence of [imath]f_{n}[/imath] to [imath]f[/imath] requires simply that at every point [imath]x[/imath] of [imath]X[/imath] the sequence [imath](f_{n}(x))[/imath] of real numbers converges to the real number [imath]f(x)[/imath]. From this you can also tell why mathematicians use a word such as "pointwise" or "punctual" to modify such a mode of convergence. On the other hand, the sequence [imath](f_{n})[/imath] is said to converge to [imath]f[/imath] uniformly iff for every [imath]\varepsilon > 0[/imath] there is some integer [imath]N \geq 1[/imath] such that [imath]n \geq N[/imath] implies [imath]d(f_{n}(x),f(x)) < \varepsilon[/imath] for all [imath]x \in X[/imath]. Now uniform convergence of [imath](f_{n})[/imath] to [imath]f[/imath] instead requires that [imath]f_{n}[/imath] should be arbitrarily close to [imath]f[/imath] if [imath]n[/imath] is large enough.
If [imath]X, M := \mathbb{R}[/imath], then intuitively graphically you can see that uniform convergence of [imath](f_{n})[/imath] to [imath]f[/imath] requires that the graph of each [imath]f_{n}[/imath] with [imath]n[/imath] large enough should be arbitrarily close to the graph of [imath]f[/imath]. However, pointwise convergence of [imath](f_{n})[/imath] to [imath]f[/imath] does not require that much.
