Tensors with conventional names?

I was wondering, what kinds of tensors (in the sense of "multi-index arrays of numbers") exist that have conventional names, which can help describing results of tensor calculations.
I can only think of two examples:
The 2nd-rank Kronecker delta defined as
[imath] \delta_{a,b} = \begin{cases}
1 & \mathrm{if}~a=b \\
0 & \mathrm{otherwise}
\end{cases} [/imath]
The arbitrary-rank Levi-Civita symbol defined as [imath]
\epsilon_{a_1,...,a_r} = \begin{cases}
+1&\text{if }(a_1,...,a_r)\text{ an even permutation of }(1,...,r)\\
-1&\text{if }(a_1,...,a_r)\text{ an odd permutation of }(1,...,r)\\
0&\text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
[/imath]
I am trying to find conventional names to express tensors I encountered in group theory. An example was a [imath]3\times3\times3[/imath] tensor [imath]A_{abc}[/imath] with 6 equal elements at the positions where [imath]a,b,c[/imath] are mutually different, which can be expressed as [imath]A_{abc} \propto |\epsilon_{abc}|[/imath], and a [imath]3\times3\times3\times3[/imath] tensor which had 9 equal non-zero elements at positions matching an index pattern [imath]aabb[/imath] (6 elements) or [imath]aaaa[/imath] (3 elemets) (assuming [imath]a\neq b[/imath]), which can be written as [imath]A_{abcd} \propto \delta_{ab} \delta_{cd}[/imath].
However, I also came across a tensor which had 21 equal non-zero elements for the patterns [imath]aaaa[/imath] (3), [imath]aabb[/imath] (6), (as above) but additionally also for [imath]abab[/imath] (6) and [imath]abba[/imath] (6). I couldn't figure out any convenient way to express this tensor, that didn't require introducing a new "tensor of ones". The closest I got was [imath]\delta_{ab}\delta_{cd}+\delta_{ac}\delta_{bd}+\delta_{ad}\delta_{bc} - 2\delta_{ab}\delta_{bc}\delta_{cd}[/imath], but that is hardly concise nor is it obvious from looking at the equation that all non-zero components are the same.
This question may be related to asking for tensors which are invariant under orthonormal basis transformations.

I haven't seen the notation [imath]\delta_{abcd}[/imath] for the quantity that is [imath]1[/imath] for [imath]a = b = c = d[/imath] and zero otherwise, but one can just write [imath]\delta_{abcd}[/imath] as [imath]\delta_{ab} \delta_{ac} \delta_{ad}.[/imath]
IIRC the only tensors which are invariant under orthogonal transformations (i.e., elements of [imath]SO(n)[/imath]) in a general linear vector space are [imath]\delta_{ab}[/imath], [imath]\epsilon_{abc\cdots}[/imath], and tensor products thereof.
If you're looking for a good exposition of invariant subspaces of tensor spaces under [imath]SO(3)[/imath] transformations, I would suggest looking in Hamermesh's Group Theory and its Applications to Physical Problems. Unfortunately I lent out my copy and don't have the time to reconstruct all of the results necessary, or I'd summarize it as an answer.

You could replace the expression in question with [imath]\delta_{ab} \delta_{ac} \delta_{ad} \delta_{bc} \delta_{bd} \delta_{bc}[/imath]. It's ugly and not very parsimonious, but at least it makes it more or less apparently that the resulting expression is symmetric in [imath]a, b, c, d[/imath].
