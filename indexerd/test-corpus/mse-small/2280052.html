Think of the complex plane. (Also, [imath]i[/imath] is neither positive nor negative.)

In the reals all non-zero numbers have a parity. Either they are positive or they are negative. [imath]\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty}|ax| =\infty[/imath] (if [imath]a \ne 0[/imath]) because the magnitude of [imath]ax[/imath] gets infinitely large.
If [imath]a > 0[/imath] then [imath]\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty}ax = \infty[/imath] because the magnitude of [imath]ax[/imath] becomes infinite and the parity of all [imath]ax[/imath] is positive.
If [imath]-a < 0[/imath] then [imath]\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty}-ax = -\infty[/imath]. What's the difference between [imath]-\infty[/imath] and [imath]\infty[/imath]? Neither of them are actual numbers. Well, again, the magnitude of [imath]-ax[/imath] becomes infinite. But the parity of all [imath]-ax[/imath] is negative so instead of increasing infinitely "in the positive direction", [imath]-ax[/imath] increase in the "negative direction". So [imath]-\infty [/imath] indicates infinite magnitude- negative parity.
Non-zero Complex numbers do not have a single bidirectional parity. A complex number has two components, a real one and an imaginary on and thus are two-dimensional and instead of having a single positive/negative parity, they have a directional angle called an argument. These angles can be in any of an infinite number of "directions" from [imath]0^{\circ}[/imath] to [imath]360^{\circ}[/imath]. The number positive [imath]1[/imath] has an argument of [imath]0^{\circ}[/imath]. Then number [imath]-1[/imath] has an argument of [imath]180^{\circ}[/imath]. The number [imath]\frac 12 + i \frac {\sqrt{3}}2[/imath] has an argument of [imath]30^{\circ}[/imath].
And [imath]i[/imath] has argument [imath]90^{\circ}[/imath].
So what happens to [imath]ix[/imath] as [imath]x \rightarrow \infty[/imath]? Well just line [imath]ax[/imath] and [imath]-ax[/imath] its magnitude increases to infinity. So [imath]\lim_{x\infty} |ix| = \infty[/imath]. But what is the argument of all the [imath]ix[/imath]? They all have an argument of [imath]90^{\circ}[/imath]. But [imath]\infty[/imath] means infinite magnitude-positive parity. And [imath]-\infty[/imath] means infinite magnitude- negative parity.
Neither of those apply for [imath]\lim_{x\infty} ix[/imath] which will have infinite magnitude - [imath]90^{\circ}[/imath] argument. How can we indicate that?

There are several different ways to describe ways to go to infinity.
I would identify these ways with different compactifications.
Compactification is a formalized way of adding points at infinity.
Let us first study the real line.
The most common compactification is the two-point compactification, in which we add [imath]+\infty[/imath] and [imath]-\infty[/imath].
This means that make a difference between infinities at the two directions, but only add one "limit point" at each end.
Another alternative is to only add one infinity (call it [imath]\tilde\infty[/imath]), and say that [imath]x_i\to\tilde\infty[/imath] whenever [imath]|x_i|\to\infty[/imath] (in the usual sense).
You can also add several infinities in both directions.
The maximal compactification (the most infinities you can consistently add) is the Stone–Čech compactification which is horribly large: there are more infinities than real numbers.
The infinity can somehow branch in a peculiar way, but I will not go any deeper here.
This is just to show that you can consider far more exotic infinities if you want to.
Let us then turn to the complex plane.
The most common compactification is the one-point one (known as the Riemann sphere), where a single infinity [imath]\tilde\infty[/imath] is added.
In this compactification [imath]ki[/imath] tends to [imath]\tilde\infty[/imath] as [imath]k\to\infty[/imath].
One alternative is a radial compactification, adding one point at each direction.
(Formally, you can take a radial diffeomorphism of the complex plane to the open unit disc, and the compactification will be the closed disc.)
In this compactification you can describe infinities as [imath]\lambda\infty[/imath], where [imath]\lambda[/imath] is a complex number of unit length.
In this compactification [imath]ki[/imath] tends to [imath]i\infty[/imath] as [imath]k\to\infty[/imath].
The complex plane also has Stone–Čech compactification.
Something yet different happens to your sequence there.
(The space [imath]\beta\mathbb C[/imath] is not sequentially compact unless I'm mistaken, and I'm not sure if this particular sequence even has a limit. But that's not even important for this answer.)
So, the limit [imath]\lim_{k\to\infty}ik[/imath] can be different things depending on how you view the infinity.
There is no single correct answer.
The same applies to [imath]i\lim_{k\to\infty}k[/imath], although multiplication does not always extend nicely to limit objects.
In the two reasonable compactifications of the complex plane I presented, multiplication makes sense and commutes with the limit.
For the radial compactification [imath]i\lim_{k\to\infty}k[/imath] is indeed [imath]i\infty[/imath], but for the one-point one it is [imath]i\tilde\infty=\tilde\infty[/imath].

The Mathematica reason: because Mathematica doesn't know any rule for how to simplify I Infinity. The mathematical reason: because there's no good way to decide what the product of the complex unit i and the symbol for infinity should mean (and further, because if you want to consider the product to be the limit of I * x as x tends to infinity, there's no preferred direction in which to let x move).
