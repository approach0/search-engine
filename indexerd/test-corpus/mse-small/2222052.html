Show that this entire function is polynomial.

Let [imath]f[/imath] be an entire function such that [imath] |f(z)| \to \infty[/imath] as [imath]|z| \to \infty[/imath]. Prove that [imath]f[/imath] is a polynomial.


Consider the holomorphic function on [imath]\mathbb{C}^*[/imath]
[imath]
g(z)=f\left(\frac{1}{z}\right).
[/imath]
So
[imath]
\lim_{z\rightarrow 0}\;|g(z)|=+\infty.
[/imath]
Obviously, [imath]0[/imath] is not a removable singularity.
Now by the Casorati-Weierstrass theorem, [imath]0[/imath] is not an essential singularity either. Indeed, take [imath]r>0[/imath] such that [imath]|g(z)|\geq 1[/imath] for all [imath]0<|z|Edit: As pointed out by @mrf, it is actually easy to see that this implies [imath]0[/imath] is a pole for [imath]g[/imath]. It suffices to use Riemann's theorem on removable singularities adequately. Indeed [imath]h(z)=1/g(z)[/imath] is holomorphic and bounded in some [imath]\{z\;;\;0<|z|So keep the Casorati-Weierstrass argument for weaker assumptions such as the ones I mention below.
So [imath]0[/imath] must be a pole. Hence the Laurent series of [imath]g[/imath] at [imath]0[/imath] is of the form
[imath]
g(z)=\frac{a_n}{z^n}+\ldots+\frac{a_1}{z}+a_0.
[/imath]
Note that there are no positive powers of [imath]z[/imath] because this comes from the power series of [imath]f[/imath].
The result follows clearly by going back to [imath]f[/imath].
Edit: Note the same proof works if we simply assume that [imath]f[/imath] is entire and there exist [imath]\alpha>0[/imath] and [imath]M>0[/imath] such that
[imath]
|f(z)|\geq \alpha\qquad \forall |z|\geq M.
[/imath]
Of course, there could be even weaker assumptions like there exists [imath]M>0[/imath] such that [imath]f(\{z\;;\; |z|>M\})[/imath] is not dense in [imath]\mathbb{C}[/imath] and the proof would still work.
