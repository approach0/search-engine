Suppose [imath]H(x)=H(y)[/imath], then [imath]h(x)-h(y) = y-x[/imath]. Since
[imath]d(h(x),h(y)) \le \delta d(y,x)[/imath], we get

I'd think of it this way: you have two real functions, one, say, [imath]f[/imath], is [imath](1 - \epsilon)[/imath]-contracting and other [imath]g[/imath] [imath](1 + \delta)[/imath]-expanding, [imath]\epsilon > 0, \delta \ge 0 [/imath] , and you want to prove injectivity of their sum. If you look at their graphs, then for each point on graph of [imath]f[/imath] will lie in a cone like this [imath]><[/imath] with apex on that point and graph of [imath]g[/imath] will lie inside vertical part of similar one, but with obtuse angle. With a bit of triangle inequality usage you'll see that [imath]C(f + g)[/imath] will be expanding for some [imath]C[/imath], expanding maps are obviously injective and multiplication is a bijection.
